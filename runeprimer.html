RUNES

Runes are the writing systems of the early Germanic languages.

The following are the runic systems, starting with the oldest, as well as their LATIN alphabet counterparts.

ELDER FUTHARK

(PROTO-GERMANIC)

rune: ᚠ ᚢ ᚦ ᚨ ᚱ ᚲ ᚷ ᚹ ᚺ ᚾ ᛁ ᛃ ᛇ ᛈ ᛉ (𐌔)
ᛊ 		ᛏ ᛒ ᛖ ᛗ ᛚ ᛜ ᛟ ᛞ
latin: f u þ a r k g w h n i j ï p z s t b e m l ŋ o d

Used in most Germanic languages, gradually abandoned starting around the Migration period (325 AD). Rule of thumb is that if the language name starts with “Old” (Old Norse, Old English, etc.), it’s too young for Elder Futhark.

FUTHORC

(ANGLO-FRISIAN, ANGLO-SAXON)

rune: ᚠ ᚢ ᚦ ᚩ ᚱ ᚳ ᚷ ᚹ ᚻ ᚾ ᛁ (ᛄ)
ᛡ 		ᛇ ᛈ ᛉ (ᚴ)
ᛋ 		ᛏ ᛒ ᛖ ᛗ ᛚ ᛝ ᛟ ᛞ ᚪ ᚫ ᚣ ᛠ ᛣ ᚸ
old english: f u þ o r c g w h n i j ï p x s t b e m l ŋ œ d a æ y ea k g

Used in Old English and Old Frisian, developed around the 400s AD. The first G rune (ᚷ) eventually began to make a "y" sound, leading to the creation of a rune that was exclusively the G sound (ᚸ). Likewise, the C rune (ᚳ) eventually began making "ch" sound, leading to the creation of a K rune (ᛣ). None of these runes make the sound we currently associate with "j", and the vowels (including the "y" you see there) are not pronounced the same as they are today.

Gradually developed from Younger Futhark from 1000 – 1200 AD and was primarily used in Scandinavia by the descendents of the Old Norse languages, and was used until runes were gradually abandoned altogether. Named Futhork after an inscription in the same vein as the others, though it appears to mostly have been written in alphabetical order, as presented here.
